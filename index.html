Properties Of Multidimensional Vector Zeckendorf Representations

Zeckendorf's Theorem gives that for all \(k \geq 3\), every nonnegative integer has a unique \(k\)-Zeckendorf representation as a sum of distinct \(k\)-bonacci numbers, where no \(k\) consecutive \(k\)-bonacci numbers are present in the representation. Anderson and Bicknell-Johnson extend this result to the multidimensional context: letting the \(k\)-bonacci vectors \(\vec{X}_i \in \Z^{k-1}\) be given by \(\vec{X}_0=\vec{0}\), \(\vec{X}_{-i}=\vec{e}_i\) for \(1 \leq i \leq k-1\), and \(\vec{X}_n=\sum_{i=1}^k \vec{X}_{n-i}\) for all \(n \in \Z\), they show that for all \(k \geq 3\), every \(\vec{v} \in \mathbb Z^{k-1}\) has a unique \(k\)-bonacci vector Zeckendorf representation, a sum of distinct \(k\)-bonacci vectors where no \(k\) consecutive \(k\)-bonacci vectors are present in the representation. Their proof provides an inductive algorithm for finding such representations. We present two improved algorithms for finding the \(k\)-bonacci vector Zeckendorf representation of \(\vec{v}\) and analyze their relative efficiency. We utilize a projection map \(S_n:\mathbb Z^{k-1} \to \mathbb Z_{\geq 0}\), introduced by Anderson, that reduces the study of \(k\)-bonacci vector representations to the setting of \(k\)-bonacci number representations, provided a lower bound is established for the most negatively indexed \(k\)-bonacci vector present in the \(k\)-bonacci vector Zeckendorf representation of \(\vec{v}\). Using this map and a bijection between \(\mathbb Z^{k-1}\) and \(\mathbb Z_{\geq 0}\), we further show that the number of and gaps between summands in \(k\)-bonacci vector Zeckendorf representations exhibit the same properties as those in \(k\)-Zeckendorf representations and that \(k\)-bonacci vector Zeckendorf representations exhibit summand minimality.

Additive number theory, analytic combinatorics, algorithms
Summer 2025 SMALL REU Paper Presented at Young Mathematicians Conference, SUMRY REU Preprint
Authors   (alphabetical order): Julian Duvivier Xiaoyao Huang Ava Kennon Say-Yeon Kwon Steven J Miller Arman Rysmakhanov Pramana Saldin Ren Watson

Comparing Left and Right Quotient Sets in Groups

Abstract: While the right and left quotient sets are equal if \(G\) is abelian, subtleties arise when \(G\) is a nonabelian group, where the cardinality difference \(|AA^{-1}| - |A^{-1}A|\) may be take on arbitrarily large values. Using the results of Martin and O'Bryant on the cardinality differences of sum sets and difference sets in \(\mathbb{Z}\), we prove in the infinite dihedral group, \(D_\infty \cong \Z \rtimes \Z/2\Z\), every integer difference is achievable. Further, we prove that in \(F_2\), the free group on \(2\) generators, an integer difference is achievable if and only if that integer is even, and we explicitly construct subsets of \(F_2\) that achieve every even integer. We further determine the minimum cardinality of \(A \subset G\) so that the difference between the cardinalities of the left and right quotient sets is nonzero, depending on the existence of order \(2\) elements in \(G\). To prove these results, we construct difference graphs \(D_A\) and \(D_{A^{-1}}\) which encode equality, respectively, in the right and left quotient sets. We observe a bijection from edges in \(D_A\) to edges in \(D_{A^{-1}}\) and count connected components in order to obtain our results on cardinality differences \(|AA^{-1}| - |A^{-1}A|\).

Group theory, combinatorics, graph theory

Lock In

LockIn is an edtech platform designed to help college students learn more efficiently by tracking lecture content, student engagement, and slide material in real time. It combines data streams from lecture audio, slides, and the student's expressed attentiveness (via Hume's expressive API) to identify and explain concepts that a student might have missed during class. Using a React frontend and Flask backend, it maps periods of low engagement to corresponding lecture segments and generates personalized study guides or strategies.

React, Flask, Python, JavaScript, LLM integration

Blackjack Engine

The engine allows the user to play Blackjack alongside the AI against a dealer following standard casino rules. At the core of the AI is an abstract class implementing the key components of MCTS, with a derived class adapting it to the specific dynamics and rules of Blackjack. On each turn, the MCTS algorithm selects the most promising game state to explore using an upper confidence bound that balances exploitation of high-performing states with exploration of less visited states. It then runs fast Monte Carlo simulations from that state to a game outcome and backpropagates the result to update its estimates for each preceding state. Over many simulations, this allows the engine to build a highly tuned decision tree for optimizing its Blackjack play. Although MCTS is definitely an overkill for Blackjack, it ultimately serves as a proof of concept and a stepping stone for developing more complex game engines.

Java, MCTS, strategy

Handwriting Tutor

This was a high school hardware project: we built a cheap device that autonomously teaches handwriting to people with visual impairment / blindness. We created a CNC-router-like technology to create a tactile learning interface and tested it with real clients. The device uses a voice UI to guide the user through the learning process, and it can be controlled via voice commands. The device was built using a Raspberry Pi, stepper motors, and 3D-printed parts. The software was written in JavaScript. We won the 1st place at a science fair (national level).
